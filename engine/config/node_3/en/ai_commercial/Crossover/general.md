# AI Agent Commercialization · Crossover Collaboration Events · Venue Selection Criteria Knowledge Base

## Scale Quick Reference

| Scale Level | Headcount | Typical Scenarios |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Boutique Crossover | 30-60 | Deep co-creation workshops, invitation-only industry roundtables |
| Standard Crossover | 60-120 | Scenario workshops, industry dialogues, multi-group parallel co-creation |
| Large-Scale Crossover | 120-200 | Crossover innovation hackathons, ecosystem conferences with co-creation sessions |

## Selection Criteria

### Criterion 1: Spatial Neutrality and Bilateral Comfort

**Definition:** Whether the venue's space type, style, and character can make participants from both the AI technology side and the vertical industry side feel comfortable and respected. This is the **core criterion** that distinguishes crossover events from all other event types—if the venue leans toward either side, the other side will feel alienated, directly impacting the quality of cross-domain exchange.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Neutrality of venue type (Innovation centers / incubators / co-working spaces > pure tech parks or pure industry association venues—the former signals "innovative collaboration" to both sides, while the latter may make one side feel like they are "on the other party's turf")
- Whether the space style is "professional yet approachable" (Too geeky/tech-forward → industry side feels out of place; too traditional/formal/governmental → tech side feels out of place; "modern, professional, well-designed but understated" is the safe choice)
- Sense of equality in the space (Avoid spatial cues suggesting one side is "on stage" and the other is "in the audience"—roundtable / island-style layouts > classroom / auditorium-style layouts)
- Quality of finishing and furnishings (Must make industry-side executives and experts feel valued, while avoiding excessive luxury that feels out of place for the tech side)
- Venue brand recognition (Venues that both sides have heard of or respect > venues recognized only within one side's circle)
- Historical event types at the venue (Venues that have previously hosted crossover / cross-industry events are more reliable; venues that have only hosted pure tech events or pure industry events carry bias risk)

---

### Criterion 2: Breakout Co-Creation Spaces

**Definition:** Whether the venue can provide multiple independent or semi-independent small-group co-creation spaces to support the core segment of crossover events—group discussions and co-creation. Crossover events typically require splitting participants into multiple mixed groups, with each group needing its own workspace for in-depth discussion and output.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Number of independently usable breakout spaces (Based on 6-10 people per group, a 60-person event requires at least 6-8 breakout spaces)
- Degree of isolation between breakout spaces (At minimum, semi-isolated—groups should not interfere with each other's discussions, but full enclosure is not necessary)
- Configuration of each breakout space: whiteboards / writable wall surfaces (essential visualization tool for crossover discussions), desk space (for laptops and materials), power outlets, independent display screens (for group output presentations)
- Ease of transition between breakout spaces and the main venue ("All-hands assembly" to "breakout discussion" transitions need to be smooth—participants should not spend 10 minutes looking for their room)
- Furniture flexibility in breakout spaces (Must accommodate both seated discussions and standing whiteboard co-creation—fixed conference tables with fixed chairs in meeting rooms are not flexible enough)
- Breakout space area (Each group of 6-10 people requires at least 15-25 m²; cramped spaces reduce discussion quality)
- If no independent breakout rooms are available, whether the main space can achieve group separation through movable partitions or furniture rearrangement (a common approach in large lofts and co-working spaces)

---

### Criterion 3: Main Venue Layout Flexibility

**Definition:** Whether the main venue space can support layout transitions across the multiple segments of a crossover event—dialogue → breakout → presentation. Crossover events do not follow a single format; they typically include plenary dialogue, group co-creation, and output presentation segments, and the space must support rapid transitions between these modes.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Whether the main venue supports multiple layout modes (roundtable dialogue, island-style breakout, theater-style presentation, etc., with the ability to switch between segments)
- Furniture mobility (Whether tables and chairs can be freely rearranged—crossover events require more frequent layout changes than most event types)
- Whether the main venue area can accommodate the target headcount under all layout configurations (Roundtable-style layouts require more floor space than theater-style; calculate based on the maximum space requirement)
- Layout requirements for "dialogue mode" (Roundtable / U-shape / dual semi-circle—emphasizing equal bilateral exchange; avoid one-directional auditorium-style layouts)
- Layout requirements for "presentation mode" (Each group needs to present their output in sequence; requires a presentation area visible to all, equipped with screens or large displays)
- Time and labor cost for layout transitions (If each layout change takes 30 minutes, the event flow will be disrupted)
- Ceiling height and sense of space (When multiple groups are discussing simultaneously, low ceilings make the space feel oppressive and noisy)

---

### Criterion 4: Audio-Visual and Collaboration Equipment

**Definition:** Whether the venue's hardware can simultaneously support dialogue sessions, group collaboration, AI product demos, and output presentations across multiple segments.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Main venue projection / large display (Used during dialogue and presentation segments)
- Sound system and microphone configuration (Roundtable dialogue segments require clear amplification; breakout segments do not require full-venue amplification, but presentation segments do)
- Independent display screens for breakout spaces (Each group needs a screen for collaboration and output presentation—this is a commonly underestimated hardware requirement for crossover events)
- Number of whiteboards / writable wall surfaces (At least 1 per group, ideally 2—visualization needs in crossover discussions are significantly higher than in homogeneous group discussions)
- Video conferencing equipment (If remote participants are involved)
- Display conditions for AI product demos (The tech side may need to demonstrate AI product applications in industry scenarios live, requiring stable network connectivity and high-definition display)

---

### Criterion 5: Network Infrastructure

**Definition:** Whether the network environment can support AI product demos, online collaboration tool usage during breakout sessions, and general internet access for all attendees. Network demand for crossover events is lower than for hackathons or Community Days (not all-day, full-capacity intensive development), but the network requirements for AI product demo segments remain high.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Total bandwidth (Must support full-venue internet access + AI product demos + potential online collaboration tool usage)
- Whether the demo area / speaker podium has dedicated wired network access (AI product demos require high network stability)
- Full-venue WiFi coverage (Breakout spaces also need coverage, not just the main venue)
- Whether there are network access restrictions (AI product API calls need unimpeded connectivity)
- Concurrent device capacity (Participants from both sides of a crossover event are likely to bring laptops)
- Whether advance network testing is permitted

---

### Criterion 6: Space Capacity

**Definition:** Whether the main venue and breakout space areas can meet the capacity requirements of a crossover event across its different segments. Space requirements for crossover events are significantly greater than for a presentation-only event of equivalent headcount, because group co-creation demands additional space.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Main venue capacity under roundtable / island-style layouts (Not theater-style maximum capacity—crossover event dialogue layouts require more floor space per person)
- Whether total breakout space area is sufficient (15-25 m² per group × number of groups)
- Refreshment / networking area size (In crossover events, refreshment breaks are important windows for informal cross-domain exchange—a narrow hallway is not adequate)
- If the main venue and breakout spaces are on the same floor or in the same zone, whether total area satisfies all functional zones
- Ceiling height (When multiple groups are discussing simultaneously, low ceilings cause significant noise buildup)

---

### Criterion 7: Transportation and Accessibility

**Definition:** How convenient it is for attendees to reach the venue. Audiences from both sides of a crossover event may come from different geographic clusters (tech side concentrated in tech parks, industry side concentrated in business districts or industry hubs), and venue selection must accommodate both.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Whether the venue location is reasonably convenient for both audiences (e.g., if the tech side is concentrated on the west side in tech parks and the industry side is concentrated on the east side in the CBD, a venue in a central location or near a transportation hub is more equitable)
- Public transit accessibility (Direct metro access preferred)
- Driving and parking conditions (Industry-side executives and experts may have a higher rate of driving)
- Ride-hailing convenience
- Venue entrance visibility and clear wayfinding signage
- Nearby lunch and dining options (Full-day events require a lunch solution)

---

### Criterion 8: Service Quality and Amenities

**Definition:** Whether the services provided by the venue can support the operational needs of a crossover event. Crossover events have high operational complexity (multi-segment transitions, group management, multiple spaces in parallel use), requiring strong cooperation from the venue operator.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Venue operator's willingness to coordinate multi-space parallel use (Breakout spaces need to open simultaneously and receive service at the same time)
- Venue operator's willingness to accommodate frequent layout changes (Dialogue → breakout → presentation transitions may require venue staff assistance)
- Catering and refreshment service capability and flexibility (Refreshment breaks at crossover events are important networking opportunities; quality must meet industry-side audience expectations)
- Zoned air conditioning controls (The main venue and breakout spaces may require different temperature settings)
- Adjustable lighting (Dialogue segments and presentation segments have different lighting requirements)
- On-site technical support (Multi-room display and audio systems may require technical staff assistance)
- Advance setup time (Crossover event space setup is more complex than typical events and requires more lead time)

---

### Criterion 9: Branding and Tone Communication

**Definition:** Whether the venue can support the organizer's branding requirements while conveying a tone of "crossover, openness, and collaboration." Branding for crossover events requires a careful balance: showcasing the organizer's AI technology brand without making the industry side feel like they have been "brought in to endorse a tech company."

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Branding installation areas (Backdrops, directional signage, group identifiers, etc.—crossover events have a more complex signage system than single-audience events, as participants from different backgrounds need clear guidance)
- "Balance" in branding visibility (If there is an industry-side co-organizer, branding placement should reflect bilateral equality; avoid having only the AI side's branding cover the entire venue)
- Photography and recording conditions (Crossover event outcomes and processes are frequently used for subsequent case study communications—photos from events like "AI × Finance Crossover Co-Creation" have high media value)
- Whether the venue's own brand conveys a tone of "innovation / crossover" (Innovation centers and incubators carry this tone inherently, while traditional hotel conference rooms require more setup to create the right atmosphere)
- Whether publishing event content on social media is permitted

## Default Priority Ranking

> **Note:** The following is a general priority ranking for the AI Agent commercialization crossover collaboration event scenario. The model should dynamically adjust based on the specific event's scale, industry focus, and event format.

**Hard Requirements (Must be met; otherwise disqualify the venue):**
1. Breakout Co-Creation Spaces — Without sufficient breakout spaces, the crossover co-creation segment cannot be executed
2. Spatial Neutrality and Bilateral Comfort — A venue biased toward either side will cause crossover exchange to fail
3. Space Capacity — Must simultaneously satisfy area requirements for both the main venue and breakout spaces
4. Main Venue Layout Flexibility — A space that cannot switch between dialogue / breakout / presentation modes cannot support a crossover event

**Nice-to-Haves (Better if met, but trade-offs are acceptable):**
5. Audio-Visual and Collaboration Equipment — Independent breakout displays and whiteboards are strong requirements, but can be partially addressed by bringing your own equipment
6. Network Infrastructure — AI product demo segments have hard requirements, but if no demo segment is planned, this can be deprioritized
7. Transportation and Accessibility — Affects attendance rates from both sides; escalates to a hard requirement when the geographic distribution gap between audiences is large
8. Service Quality and Amenities — Importance increases when industry-side audience expectations are high
9. Branding and Tone Communication — Affects media and communications impact but does not affect the core event experience

## Hybrid Scenario Handling Guidelines

When crossover collaboration events are combined with other event types (e.g., "morning industry dialogue + afternoon co-creation workshop" or "crossover event + evening networking reception"), the model should:

1. Take the **union** of criteria from each event type's knowledge base
2. For shared criteria, apply the **higher standard**
3. Special considerations for common combinations:
   - **Crossover Dialogue + Co-Creation Workshop**: The dialogue segment requires a roundtable layout for equal exchange; the workshop segment requires hands-on working conditions. The space must support transitions from "seated discussion" to "hands-on group work"
   - **Crossover Event + Networking Reception**: Daytime professional collaboration atmosphere → evening relaxed networking atmosphere. The reception segment of a crossover event is extremely valuable (it breaks the formality of daytime structured exchange); the feasibility of space transition must be evaluated
   - **Large-Scale Crossover Innovation Hackathon**: Essentially a crossover version of a hackathon. Requires layering a hackathon's endurance capabilities (power, network, catering) with crossover event breakout co-creation space requirements—this places the highest overall demands on the venue
