# Web3 · Community Day (Builder Co-Creation Day) · Venue Selection Criteria Knowledge Base


## Scale Quick Reference

| Scale Level | Headcount | Typical Scenarios |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Small Co-Creation | 10-25 | Core Builder closed-door co-creation, grant project collaboration |
| Standard Community Day | 25-50 | Regular community Builder Day, city tour series |
| Large Community Day | 50-100 | Builder gathering during summit, multi-project joint co-creation |

---

## Selection Criteria

### Criterion 1: Network Reliability

**Definition:** Whether the network environment can support all Builders simultaneously conducting on-chain development, smart contract deployment, wallet interactions, API calls, and other high-intensity tasks throughout the entire day. Web3 development demands more than just bandwidth—it also involves specialized access requirements.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Total bandwidth (must support all developers simultaneously performing on-chain interactions, contract deployment, frontend development, and other mixed traffic)
- Wi-Fi coverage range and signal strength (venue-wide with no dead zones)
- Network stability (8-10 hours of continuous use with no periodic disconnections—an interrupted on-chain transaction submission could result in financial loss)
- Concurrent device capacity (Builders carry at minimum one laptop + one smartphone each, with some requiring additional hardware wallet connections)
- **Critical: Network access restrictions** (Web3 development is highly dependent on specific network conditions—certain RPC nodes, IPFS gateways, blockchain explorers, DEX frontends, and similar services may be blocked by the venue's or region's network. This must be confirmed in advance)
- Whether connecting personal routers or VPN devices is permitted
- Whether wired network access points are available (for use in the technical support area/demo zone)
- 4G/5G signal coverage (as an emergency backup)
- **On-site testing is mandatory**: Bring devices to the venue in advance and test the actual accessibility of mainstream on-chain tools (MetaMask connectivity, contract deployment, RPC calls, etc.)

---

### Criterion 2: Power Outlet Density

**Definition:** Whether the venue can provide sufficient charging capacity for all Builders to sustain a full day of development work. Web3 developers' device power consumption is comparable to AI developers, making power requirements equally critical.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Existing outlet count and distribution (count the actual number of usable outlets)
- Outlet-to-seat ratio (ideal: at least 1 usable outlet per 2-3 seats)
- Whether power strips/extension cords are permitted
- Cable routing safety after power strip placement (whether floor cables create tripping hazards)
- Total circuit load capacity of the venue
- Charging stations/portable power banks as supplementary options

---

### Criterion 3: Spatial Comfort & All-Day Endurance

**Definition:** Whether the environment makes Builders willing and able to comfortably stay for an entire day. Web3 Builders typically have higher expectations for a "relaxed vibe" than AI developers—the Web3 community culture rejects the feeling of being "managed," and the space needs to make people "want to stay" rather than "have to stay."

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Seating comfort (suitable for extended coding sessions—avoid purely decorative seating)
- Desktop space (at least 60 cm width per person to accommodate laptop + mouse + water bottle + potential hardware wallet)
- Desk-to-chair height compatibility (ergonomic height suitable for typing)
- Natural light conditions (sufficient but not glaring directly onto screens)
- Indoor temperature controllability
- Ventilation conditions
- Ambient noise level ("moderately quiet"—Web3 Builder Days typically involve high-frequency discussion, so the space must accommodate free-flowing conversation)
- Restroom count and accessibility
- Nearby lunch accessibility (dining options within a 5-minute walk, or food delivery permitted on-site)
- International attendee convenience (English menu/ordering options available; staff capable of basic English communication—Web3 communities have a high proportion of international participants)

---

### Criterion 4: Spatial Ambiance & Community Alignment

**Definition:** Whether the venue's visual style and spatial character match the cultural aesthetic of the Web3 Builder community. The Web3 community is highly sensitive to venue tone—a venue that "feels right" will be organically shared across the community, while one that "feels off" will be criticized.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Overall space style and Web3 compatibility (independent café/creative space/loft/co-working space > chain business café/standard meeting room/hotel business center)
- Photo-friendliness and distribution value (Builders naturally share "today's coding venue" on Twitter/Farcaster—the venue needs visual appeal)
- The space's "relaxed vibe" and "underground feel" (Web3 Builder community preference: distinctive independent spaces > polished but standardized corporate environments)
- Whether the space is "too formal" (a buttoned-up corporate setting will make the Builder crowd uncomfortable)
- Whether the space is "too cheap" (a purely functional space has no alignment issues but also has no distribution value)
- Whether the space has "local character" (when touring different cities, choosing a venue that the local Builder community already knows or that has distinctive local flavor brings built-in community appeal)
- The venue's existing recognition within the Web3/tech community (venues that have previously hosted similar events have a stronger trust foundation)

---

### Criterion 5: Spatial Layout & Functional Zoning

**Definition:** Whether the space can naturally divide into work zones, discussion zones, technical support areas, and other functional areas. Web3 Community Days typically involve high-density interaction (spontaneous technical discussions between Builders, collaborative debugging, etc.), so the space must simultaneously support both "quiet coding" and "lively discussion" modes.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Whether the main work area can seat the target headcount for simultaneous work
- Whether natural functional zoning exists (quiet development area + open discussion area + technical support corner—Web3 Builder Days typically have higher discussion frequency than AI equivalents, requiring a larger proportion of "discussion space")
- Setup conditions for a technical support corner (supporting 1-on-1 or small group collaborative debugging, with desktop space and stable network)
- Check-in/materials station placement
- Tea break/coffee station placement (as a social gathering point)
- Furniture flexibility (movable tables and chairs to accommodate spontaneous team-up discussions—Web3 Builders frequently form impromptu collaboration groups)
- Seating variety (proper work desks + sofa areas + high-top bar seating—different Builders have different work style preferences)
- If outdoor areas are available (terrace, courtyard), whether they can serve as overflow space for "fresh air + casual conversation"

---

### Criterion 6: Space Capacity

**Definition:** How many people the venue can actually accommodate for comfortable simultaneous work in Community Day mode.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Actual workable seat count (not the venue's stated maximum capacity—each person needs a full desktop workstation)
- Whether per-person area is adequate (recommended ≥ 2.5 m² per person)
- Tolerance for attendance fluctuation (Web3 Community Days have a come-and-go culture, and international attendees' time zone differences lead to more staggered arrival times—the space should not feel overcrowded at peak or empty during lulls)
- If the venue is partially reserved, whether the boundary between reserved and non-reserved areas is clearly defined

---

### Criterion 7: Transportation & Accessibility

**Definition:** How convenient it is for attendees to reach the venue. Web3 Community Days have a high proportion of international attendees who are unfamiliar with local transportation, so venue accessibility requires additional consideration for this factor.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Whether the venue location is easy to describe and navigate to for international attendees (near a well-known landmark > deep inside a hidden alley)
- Public transit accessibility (direct metro/bus access)
- Ride-hailing convenience
- Venue entrance visibility and signage clarity (English signage is important for international attendees)
- If this is a Builder Day during a summit, distance from the main summit venue and primary attendee hotels
- Whether the surrounding area is a recognized Builder community/tech community hub ("that neighborhood" recognition factor)

---

### Criterion 8: Venue Collaboration Flexibility

**Definition:** How accommodating the venue is toward the non-standard usage pattern of a Community Day—"all-day occupation, bring-your-own equipment, international crowd gathering."

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Full-venue or partial reservation policies and pricing structure
- Time flexibility (Web3 Community Days sometimes run from morning through evening or later, requiring high schedule elasticity)
- Advance setup time (at least 1-2 hours for setting up power strips, materials, and network testing)
- Permission for bringing in personal equipment and materials (power strips, branded collateral, table cards, etc.)
- Permission for photography and social media posting (the Web3 community will almost certainly take extensive photos for social media)
- Tolerance for discussion noise (Web3 Builders tend to generate high-energy conversation)
- Dining flexibility (whether food delivery is permitted; whether venue food and beverage purchases are mandatory)
- International attendee reception capability (basic English communication, diverse payment method support)
- Willingness to collaborate on recurring/series events

---

### Criterion 9: Branding Conditions

**Definition:** Whether the venue supports the organizer's lightweight brand presence and community distribution needs. Branding at Web3 Community Days is typically very minimal—community culture has a natural resistance to "over-branding," but basic project identification and content creation assets are still necessary.

**Evaluation Areas:**
- Whether placing check-in stations, table cards, project stickers, and other lightweight materials is permitted (note Web3 community aesthetics: minimalist/geek style > large-scale corporate brand installations)
- Whether event photography/video is permitted
- Whether the venue's visual backdrop is suitable for producing on-brand event photos
- Whether the venue's own brand identity is compatible with the organizer's brand
- Calibrating the "degree" of brand presence (the Web3 Builder community is highly sensitive to feeling "marketed to"—brand presence should be restrained, organic, and never overpowering)

## Default Priority Ranking

> **Note:** The following is a general priority ranking for Web3 Community Day scenarios. The model should dynamically adjust based on the specific event's scale, audience, and objectives.

**Hard Requirements (must be met; otherwise disqualify immediately):**
1. Network Reliability — Builders code on-chain all day; if the network fails or on-chain tools are inaccessible, the entire event is a write-off
2. Power Outlet Density — without power for laptops, people leave by midday
3. Spatial Comfort & All-Day Endurance — a venue that cannot sustain a full-day stay is unsuitable for a Community Day
4. Space Capacity — if people cannot be seated, the event cannot proceed

**Value-Adds (better if met; can be weighed against trade-offs):**
5. Spatial Ambiance & Community Alignment — the Web3 community is highly sensitive to venue tone, affecting distribution and reputation; however, this can be weighed against trade-offs as long as functional requirements are met
6. Spatial Layout & Functional Zoning — natural zoning is preferable, but open spaces can be adapted through furniture arrangement
7. Transportation & Accessibility — importance increases with a higher proportion of international attendees; escalates to a hard requirement in summit co-located scenarios
8. Venue Collaboration Flexibility — affects implementation difficulty but can be resolved through communication and budget
9. Branding Conditions — Web3 Community Day reputation is primarily driven by organic Builder sharing; heavy brand installations should actually be kept restrained

## Hybrid Scenario Handling Guidelines

When a Web3 Community Day is combined with other event formats (e.g., "Community Day + hackathon warm-up" or "Builder Day during summit + evening side event"), the model should:

1. Take the **union** of criteria from each scenario's knowledge base
2. For overlapping criteria, apply the **higher standard**
3. Special considerations for common combinations:
   - **Community Day + Hackathon Warm-Up**: The Builder Day serves as a "warm-up" before the hackathon, requiring more collaborative discussion space (team formation/brainstorming); network demands are higher (may involve testnet deployment and contract debugging)
   - **Builder Day During Summit + Evening Side Event**: Daytime work mode → evening social mode space transition; if using the same venue, assess turnover feasibility; if using different venues, assess transit distance
   - **Multi-Project Joint Community Day**: Each project team needs its own technical support area and brand presence space, increasing the spatial zoning requirements
