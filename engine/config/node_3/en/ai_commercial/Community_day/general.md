# AI Agent Commercialization · Community Day (Developer Co-Creation Day) · Venue Selection Criteria Knowledge Base

## Capacity Quick Reference

| Scale | Headcount | Typical Scenario |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Small Co-Creation | 10–25 | Core user deep engagement, product beta testing feedback |
| Standard Community Day | 25–50 | Regular community events, city tour series |
| Large Community Day | 50–80 | Flagship events in key cities, product launch tie-ins |

---

## Selection Criteria

### Criterion 1: Network Infrastructure

**Definition:** Whether the network environment can support all developers simultaneously using AI coding tools, calling APIs, and running code throughout a full day of intensive work. This is the **#1 priority** for Community Day venues — if the network fails, the event is dead on arrival.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Total bandwidth (must support all developers using AI-assisted coding tools simultaneously — tools like Cursor/Copilot generate continuous API calls, creating traffic patterns fundamentally different from standard internet browsing)
- WiFi coverage and signal strength (full-venue, zero dead zones — no "losing connection when seated in the corner" situations)
- Network stability (sustained use over 8–10 hours with no periodic disconnections or speed degradation)
- Concurrent device capacity (developers carry at least 1 laptop + 1 phone each; a 50-person event = 100+ simultaneous connections)
- Network access restrictions (some venues/tech parks block specific ports or overseas API access — confirm that all API services required by AI products are accessible)
- Permission to connect external routers or hotspot devices (as supplementary or backup solutions)
- Availability of wired network access points (engineer Q&A area / demo area can use wired connections for guaranteed stability)
- 4G/5G signal coverage (as emergency backup)
- **On-site testing is mandatory**: Bring devices to the venue and test actual connectivity with mainstream AI coding tools — do not rely solely on the bandwidth figures quoted by the venue

---

### Criterion 2: Power Supply Density

**Definition:** Whether the venue can provide sufficient charging access for all developers to sustain full-day laptop use. In a developer's all-day work mode, insufficient power = mass exodus by mid-afternoon.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Number and distribution of existing outlets (count the actual usable outlets, not just how many wall panels are visible)
- Outlet-to-seat ratio (ideal: at least 1 usable outlet per 2–3 seats)
- Permission to use power strips / extension cords (nearly every Community Day requires supplementary power strips)
- Cable routing safety after power strip deployment (floor cables that create trip hazards; whether cable covers or tape are needed)
- Total circuit load capacity (whether the combined wattage of 50 laptops + phone chargers + power strips falls within circuit limits)
- Charging stations / shared power banks (as supplementary options)

---

### Criterion 3: Comfort & All-Day Endurance

**Definition:** Whether the space allows developers to comfortably stay for an entire day (6–10 hours). This is not a 2-hour meeting — it's an all-day immersive work session where the impact of seating comfort, lighting, temperature, and noise is amplified over time.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Seating comfort (suitable for extended typing — café bar stools are fine for 2 hours but unbearable after 8)
- Desk space (minimum 60cm width per person for laptop + mouse + water bottle; long tables are preferable to small round tables)
- Desk-chair height alignment (ergonomic height suitable for typing, not decorative low coffee tables or high bar counters)
- Natural lighting (ample daylight without direct glare on screens — near windows with shading is ideal)
- Indoor temperature control (all-day events span morning-to-evening temperature shifts; independent climate control is needed)
- Ventilation (dozens of people working in an enclosed space all day — poor airflow noticeably impacts energy and focus)
- Ambient noise level (needs to be "moderately quiet" — conversation and discussion are welcome, but not so loud that coding focus is impossible)
- Restroom count and accessibility
- Nearby lunch options (all-day events must address lunch — dining options within a 5-minute walk, or venue supports catering / food delivery)

---

### Criterion 4: Atmosphere & Developer Aesthetic

**Definition:** Whether the venue's visual style and spatial character align with the developer community's aesthetic preferences, making participants feel "this is my kind of place" rather than "I got dragged to a corporate meeting." The venue's vibe directly influences attendees' willingness to share on social media and overall community reputation.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Does the overall style match a developer/creator aesthetic? (specialty café / creative space / loft > standard meeting room / hotel business center / government training facility)
- Photo-friendliness (does the space have visual character that encourages social media sharing? — "coding with Cursor at XX café with a group of devs" has far more organic reach than "sitting in some conference room")
- Sense of relaxed informality (Community Day emphasizes openness and low barriers to entry; the space should not feel rigid or corporate)
- Design details and material quality (specialty café aesthetics > functional office space aesthetics)
- Venue "buzz factor" or memorability (locally renowned cafés, iconic creative districts, or other venues with built-in word-of-mouth value)
- Avoid "overdone" venues (developer communities feel a disconnect with overly corporate or luxury spaces)

---

### Criterion 5: Spatial Layout & Functional Zoning

**Definition:** Whether the space can naturally divide into work zones, social areas, Q&A corners, and other functional areas to support Community Day's hybrid model of "independent work + organic interaction + on-demand mentoring."

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Can the main work area seat the target headcount for simultaneous work? (each person needs desk space + chair + power access)
- Are there naturally formed functional zones? (not just one wide-open floor — there should be "quiet coding areas" and "conversation-friendly areas" coexisting)
- Setup conditions for an engineer Q&A corner (a relatively quiet area for 1-on-1 or small group discussions, but not fully isolated from the main space)
- Check-in / swag table placement (near the entrance, without disrupting the main work area)
- Coffee / refreshment station placement (should naturally create a social gathering point, but the foot traffic path should not cut through the quiet work zone)
- If an optional demo showcase area is needed: requires a dedicated screen, stable network, and space for a small audience
- Furniture flexibility (can tables and chairs be rearranged to adapt to different headcounts and zoning needs?)
- Variety of seating options (some prefer proper desks and chairs for coding, others prefer lounging on sofas — more variety is better)

---

### Criterion 6: Venue Capacity

**Definition:** How many people the venue can actually accommodate for comfortable, simultaneous work in Community Day mode.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Actual working seats (not the venue's stated maximum capacity — Community Day requires significantly more space per person than conference or networking modes, as each attendee needs a full desk workstation)
- Adequate per-person space (recommended ≥2.5 m² per person for Community Day work mode; below this density, comfort and social space are compromised)
- Tolerance for attendance fluctuation (Community Day has a come-and-go format with significant on-site headcount variation throughout the day — peak times shouldn't feel overcrowded, and off-peak times shouldn't feel empty)
- If booking a partial venue rather than full-venue: are the boundaries between reserved and non-reserved areas clearly defined?

---

### Criterion 7: Transportation & Accessibility

**Definition:** How convenient it is for attendees to reach the venue. Community Day participants typically travel from home or the office, and for an all-day event, transportation convenience directly affects willingness to attend and stay.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Distance from developer-concentrated areas (tech parks, university districts, startup neighborhoods, etc.)
- Public transit accessibility (direct metro/bus access)
- Ease of finding the venue (navigation accuracy, entrance visibility — many cafés and creative spaces are tucked away in alleys and hard to find on a first visit)
- Nearby parking options (a significant portion of developers drive)
- For city tour events: does the venue carry "local significance"? (choosing a venue that the local developer community knows or aspires to visit adds built-in draw)

---

### Criterion 8: Venue Partnership Flexibility

**Definition:** The venue's willingness to accommodate Community Day's non-standard usage model of "all-day occupation, BYOD, large group gathering." Most cafés and co-working spaces are not designed for this type of event, so flexibility in partnership terms directly determines whether the event can be successfully executed.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Booking policy (full-venue buyout vs. partial-area reservation vs. minimum spend offset — different models have different impacts on event flexibility)
- Time-of-use flexibility (Community Day typically requires full-day access from 9:00–18:00 or longer; confirm the venue's operating hours cover this)
- Early setup time (check-in tables, power strip cabling, swag placement, etc. require at least 1–2 hours of advance setup)
- Permission for external equipment and materials (power strips, roll-up banners, table cards, branded collateral)
- Permission for photography and social media posting
- Tolerance for group conversation noise (some cafés are sensitive to "a crowd of people talking")
- F&B flexibility (is purchasing from the venue's menu mandatory, or are outside lunches / food delivery permitted?)
- Willingness to partner on recurring events (Community Day is typically an ongoing series, not a one-off)

---

### Criterion 9: Brand Presence Conditions

**Definition:** Whether the venue supports the organizer's lightweight brand visibility and event content needs. Brand presence at Community Day is typically lighter than at summits or receptions, but basic brand signage and content capture capabilities are still required.

**Evaluation Checklist:**
- Permission to place check-in tables, table cards, branded standees, and other lightweight materials
- Permission to capture event photos/videos for brand promotion
- Whether the venue's visual backdrop produces high-quality event photography ("a group of developers coding in a beautiful café" has far more shareability than "people staring at laptops in a nondescript space")
- Brand compatibility between the venue and the organizer (hosting events at a well-known specialty café can create mutual brand uplift)
- Potential for co-branding partnerships (in recurring event series, joint brand promotion between the venue and the organizer adds long-term value)

## Default Priority Ranking

> **Note:** The following is a general priority ranking for AI Agent Commercialization Community Day scenarios. The model should dynamically adjust based on the specific event's scale, audience, and objectives.

**Hard Requirements (must be met; otherwise disqualify immediately):**
1. Network Infrastructure — Developers use AI coding tools all day; poor network makes the entire event worthless
2. Power Supply Density — No power for laptops means people leave by mid-afternoon
3. Comfort & All-Day Endurance — A venue that can't sustain a full day of attendance is unsuitable for Community Day
4. Venue Capacity — If it can't seat everyone, don't hold the event

**Nice-to-Haves (better if met, but negotiable):**
5. Atmosphere & Developer Aesthetic — Directly impacts social media reach and community reputation, but can be weighed against functional requirements
6. Spatial Layout & Functional Zoning — Natural zoning is ideal, but open spaces can be adapted through furniture arrangement
7. Transportation & Accessibility — Affects attendance rate, but developer communities have higher tolerance for "worth the trip" venues
8. Venue Partnership Flexibility — Affects execution difficulty, but can be resolved through negotiation and budget
9. Brand Presence Conditions — A bonus; Community Day reputation is primarily built through organic attendee sharing, not elaborate setups

## Hybrid Scenario Guidelines

When Community Day is combined with other formats (e.g., "Morning: Community Day + Afternoon: Workshop," or "Community Day + Product Beta Launch"), the model should:

1. Pull criteria from each scenario's knowledge base as a **union set**
2. For overlapping criteria, apply the **higher standard**
3. Special considerations for common combinations:
   - **Community Day + Workshop**: Requires transitioning from "open work mode" to "structured session with speakers and an agenda"; the space must support on-the-fly reconfiguration into a stage-facing layout. AV requirements (projector / large display / microphone) are unnecessary for a standalone Community Day but become hard requirements in this combination.
   - **Community Day + Product Beta Testing**: Network infrastructure requirements escalate further (beta products may call non-public APIs); a more isolated demo area is needed, and privacy requirements increase (beta content should not be exposed externally).
